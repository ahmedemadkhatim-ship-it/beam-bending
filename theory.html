Bending Stress Principle

The bending stress equation defines how a beam responds to bending moment. Stress increases with moment and distance from the neutral axis while decreasing with a larger section moment of inertia.

TermDescription
σBending stress in MPa (N/mm²)
MBending moment in N·mm
yDistance from neutral axis in mm
ISecond moment of area in mm⁴

Variable definitions

  • M — Applied bending moment. For beams under loads, M is the internal moment resisting bending.
  • y — Distance from the neutral axis. The outermost fiber sees the greatest bending stress.
  • I — Moment of inertia of the cross-section. A larger I means a stiffer, stronger section.

Using N·mm and mm produces stress in N/mm², which is numerically equal to MPa, a standard unit for material stress.

Engineering assumptions

Modern beam analysis assumes linear elastic material behavior, small deflections, and that the cross-section remains plane after bending.

  • Material is homogeneous and isotropic.
  • Beam experiences pure bending or bending-dominant loading.
  • Shear deformation is negligible for slender beams.
